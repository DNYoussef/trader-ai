Abstract Numerical interpolation methods are essential for the estimation of
nonlinear functions and they have a wide range of applications in economics and
accounting. In this regard, the idea of using interpolation methods based on mul-
tiplicativecalculusforsuitableaccountingproblemsisself-evident.Thepurposeof
thisstudy,therefore,istodevelopawaytobetterestimatethelearningcurve,which
is an exponentially decreasing function, based on multiplicative Lagrange inter-
polation. The results of this study show that the proposed multiplicative method of
learning curve provides more accurate estimates of labour costs when compared to
the conventional methods. This is because the exponential functions are linear in
multiplicative calculus. Furthermore, the results reveal that using the proposed
method enables cost and managerial accountants to better calculate both cost of
unused capacity and product cost in a cumulative production represented by a
nonlinear function. The results of this study are also expected to help researchers,
practitioners, economists, business managers, and cost and managerial accountants
to understand how to construct a multiplicative based learning curve to improve
such decisions as pricing, profit planning, capacity management, and budgeting.

1 Introduction
Alearningcurveisamathematicalfunctionthatmeasureshowtheamountofinput,
whichismainlydirectlabourhoursneededtoproduceoneunitofoutput,decreases
as quantity of output produced increases (Nadeau et al. 2010). The learning curve,
which emphasizes thatindividualsbecomemoreefficient atdoingataskwhenitis
performed repeatedly, is one of the most popular and generally accepted cost
estimation theories (Womer and Patterson 1983; Jarkas and Horner 2011).
Identifying how costs vary in accordance with the output level is essential in a
decision making process. However, since the costs act in different patterns under
different conditions, predicting them is a very difficult task. Managers can use the
past data to predict the costs that are expected to be incurred. An increase in
efficiency of the labour force, on the other hand, may result in misleading cost
predictions. This effect, in the literature, is known as the ‘Learning Effect’ (Drury
2012). Thus, the learning effect is a vital concept for understanding and estimating
the costs in an accurate way. In this regard, with better understanding of learning
effect,directlabourandproductioncostscanbecomputedmoreaccurately.This,in
turn, helps managers to make business decisions, such as cost and capacity
management, pricing, and human resource planning, more efficiently.
There has been an increasing interest in the application of the learning effect in
accounting and industrial settings (Liao 1982; Jaber and Guiffrida 2008). The
learning effect can be applied to various industries, such as aircraft, aerospace,
assembly, automotive, electronics, shipbuilding, construction, defence, and
petroleum refining, in which the activities are repetitious and identical (Steven
1999; Jarkas and Horner 2011).
Thelearningcurve,whichhasagreatpracticalimportance,couldbeausefultool
for decision makers (Fioretti 2007a, b). Accordingly, there has been a stream of
researchinvestigatingthelearningeffectinproductcostevaluation,productpricing,
profit planning, and control (e.g. Wright 1936; Harvey 1976; Mclntyre 1977; Liao
1982). To illustrate, Lieberman (1984) used the data on 37 chemical products and
found a strong and consistent learning effect in the chemical industry. In another
study, O¨ren and Erol (2009) analyzed the role of learning in reducing the cost of
production and concluded that learning should be considered as a tool that reduces
the cost of a product or service. Additionally, McKee and McKee (2013) explored
theeffectofthelearningonrevenuerecognition.Theauthorsnotethatthedifferent
amounts of revenue might be recognized when the learning effect is taken into
account under the revenue recognition standard. Togo (2001), on the other hand,
emphasized that the log-linear function should be used for learning curve because
all cost behaviour is not linear. Apart from these studies, others have investigated
the applicability of the learning curve in various sectors ranging from the aircraft
industry to the food industry (e.g. Hartley 1965; Reis 1991; Liao 2009; Jarkas and

4 Estimation of learning curve via multiplicative calculus
As stated by McDonald (1987), cost data may not be available for each unit of
product produced for several reasons such as two or more products are produced
simultaneously or the cost contract is related to the whole production rather than a
particular product. The learning curve estimation is therefore needed. To better
understandthisissue,consideracompanyinwhichtheproductiontakesplaceafter
an order has been received. In order to estimate costs by using the learning curve
phenomenon, all values should be recorded. However, because of the irregular
characteristics of the orders, some of intermediate values of cumulative production
cannotberecorded.Forinstance,assumethatthenumberoforderonthefirstdayis
20 whereas it is 50 on the second day. If the company attempts to calculate the
labourcosttobeincurredforproducingtheorderofthirddaywhichis30 units,the
company should estimate the learning effect of this specific intermediate value
based on the observed data of the 20th and 50th units. However, the conventional
learning curve estimations do not produce accurate results since the learning curve
equation is a decreasing exponential function. In this respect, multiplicative based
method is likely to produce more accurate results since it provides a better fit than
the conventional nonlinear methods.

will be considered. The first example was taken from the article written by Peles
